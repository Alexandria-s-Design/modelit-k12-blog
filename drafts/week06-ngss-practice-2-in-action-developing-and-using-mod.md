## The Challenge: Moving Beyond Rote Memorization

Traditional science education often relies heavily on rote memorization and passive learning. Students are presented with facts and figures, but rarely have the opportunity to truly explore and understand the underlying concepts. This approach can lead to a superficial understanding of science, leaving students unable to apply their knowledge to real-world problems. One of the biggest challenges is the lack of opportunities for students to engage in authentic scientific practices. Many classrooms lack the resources and support needed to implement hands-on activities and inquiry-based learning experiences. Furthermore, teachers may feel overwhelmed by the prospect of incorporating complex modeling activities into their already packed curriculum. The NGSS framework emphasizes the importance of three-dimensional learning, which integrates disciplinary core ideas, science and engineering practices, and crosscutting concepts. However, many educators struggle to effectively implement this approach in their classrooms. This can result in a disconnect between the curriculum and the students' learning experiences, hindering their ability to develop a deep and meaningful understanding of science.

## Practical Implementation: Bringing Modeling to Your Classroom

Implementing NGSS Practice 2 in your classroom doesn't have to be daunting. Start by introducing simple modeling activities that are relevant to your curriculum. For example, students can create physical models of cells, ecosystems, or the solar system. As students become more comfortable with modeling, you can introduce more complex activities using digital tools like Cell Collective. Cell Collective allows students to explore pre-built models or create their own, fostering a deeper understanding of scientific concepts. When designing modeling activities, it's important to provide students with clear guidelines and support. Encourage them to collaborate, share ideas, and revise their models based on evidence and feedback. Facilitate discussions about the strengths and limitations of different models, helping students to understand the nature of scientific inquiry. Consider integrating modeling activities into your existing lesson plans. For example, after introducing a new concept, have students create a model to explain how it works. This will help them to consolidate their understanding and apply their knowledge in a meaningful way. Remember to assess students' understanding of modeling concepts, not just their ability to create a model. Ask them to explain their reasoning, justify their choices, and evaluate the effectiveness of their models.
